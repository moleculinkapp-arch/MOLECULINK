London Dispersion Forces

The weakest IMF, present in ALL molecules. Caused by temporary dipoles from electron cloud fluctuations.

▸Present in all molecules
▸Increases with molecular size
▸Only force in nonpolar molecules

Dipole-Dipole Forces

Attractions between positive end of one polar molecule and negative end of another.

▸Only in polar molecules
▸Stronger than LDF (same size)
▸Alignment of δ⁺ to δ⁻

Hydrogen Bonding

Special strong dipole-dipole force. H bonded to N, O, or F attracted to lone pair on another N, O, or F.

▸Strongest IMF (among van der Waals)
▸H must be bonded to N, O, or F
▸Gives water its unique properties

🎯 Lab Missions A–E

Complete each mission step-by-step to master intermolecular forces.

Mission A: Building H₂O & Hydrogen Bonds

1

Build One H₂O Molecule

Take 1 red ball (O), 2 white balls (H). Use sticks to connect them in a bent/angular arrangement.

2

Identify: Sticks = Covalent Bonds

These sticks represent intramolecular forces (bonds WITHIN the molecule). They are very strong.

3

Build a Second H₂O Molecule

Repeat: 1 red ball, 2 white balls, connected with sticks.

4

Connect with Green Velcro (H-Bond)

Place molecules near each other. Connect using green Velcro strip = Hydrogen Bonding.

5

Compare the Two Types

Sticks: Hold atoms together within (STRONG). Velcro: Attracts molecules together (WEAK).

✓ Key Conclusion

Intramolecular forces hold atoms together inside molecules. Intermolecular forces attract neighboring molecules.

Mission B: CH₄ & London Dispersion Forces

1

Build Two CH₄ Molecules

Per molecule: 1 black ball (C), 4 white balls (H). Connect with sticks in tetrahedral shape.

2

Identify Polarity

Methane is nonpolar (symmetric). No partial charges.

3

Connect with Gray Velcro (LDF)

Use gray Velcro strips. This is the weakest IMF — temporary induced dipoles.

4

Test the Strength

Pull molecules apart. Gray Velcro separates VERY easily. Note how weak LDF are!

Mission C: HCl & Dipole-Dipole Forces

1

Build Two HCl Molecules

Per molecule: 1 white ball (H), 1 purple ball (Cl). Connect with 1 stick each.

2

Label δ+ and δ-

Mark H as δ+ and Cl as δ-. Cl is more electronegative.

3

Arrange: δ+ facing δ-

Position so H(δ+) of one faces Cl(δ-) of another.

4

Connect with Orange Velcro

Orange Velcro = Dipole-Dipole attraction. Moderate strength.

5

Test Wrong Orientation

Reverse one molecule: δ+ faces δ+ = Repulsion! Alignment matters.

Mission D: H₂O Hydrogen Bonding Network

1

Build Three H₂O Molecules

Per molecule: 1 red (O), 2 white (H), connected with sticks in bent shape.

2

Identify H & Lone Pairs

Each water has 2 H atoms (δ+) and 2 lone pairs on O (δ-).

3

Form a Triangular H-Bond Network

Connect H of one water to O of another using green Velcro. Form a triangle with all three.

4

Test the Strength

Green Velcro is MUCH stronger than gray or orange. This explains water's high BP and surface tension.

Mission E: IMF & Physical Properties

1

Gather All Models

Collect CH₄ (gray Velcro), HCl (orange), and H₂O (green) pairs.

2

Pull Test: Weakest to Strongest

Pull apart each pair. Order: Gray (easiest) → Orange → Green (hardest).

3

Correlate to Boiling Points

CH₄ (-161°C) < HCl (-85°C) < H₂O (100°C). Stronger IMF = Higher BP!

4

Conclusions

Strong IMF → Higher BP, MP, surface tension, lower vapor pressure. Weak IMF → opposite!

✓ Mission E Complete

You can now predict physical properties based on IMF type and strength!
